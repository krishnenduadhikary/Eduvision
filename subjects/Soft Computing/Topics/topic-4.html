1. Introduction to Genetic Algorithms & Optimization

A Genetic Algorithm (GA) is a search-based optimization technique based on the principles of Genetics and Natural Selection. It is frequently used to find optimal or near-optimal solutions to difficult problems which otherwise would take a lifetime to solve. GAs are a subset of a much larger branch of computation known as Evolutionary Computation.

Developed by John Holland and his colleagues (notably David E. Goldberg), GAs have been successfully applied to various optimization problems in research and machine learning.

Core Idea:

In GAs, we maintain a pool or population of possible solutions (individuals or chromosomes) to the given problem. These solutions then undergo recombination (crossover) and mutation (like in natural genetics), producing new children. Each individual (candidate solution) is assigned a fitness value (based on its objective function value). Fitter individuals are given a higher chance to mate and yield more "fitter" individuals. This is in line with the Darwinian Theory of "Survival of the Fittest."

This process of "evolving" better individuals continues over generations until a stopping criterion is met (e.g., a certain number of generations, or a satisfactory solution found).

Optimization is the process of making something better. In any process, we have a set of inputs and a set of outputs.

Basic Input-Process-Output model for optimization.

Optimization refers to finding the values of inputs in such a way that we get the "best" output values. The definition of "best" varies from problem to problem, but in mathematical terms, it refers to maximizing or minimizing one or more objective functions by varying the input parameters.

The set of all possible solutions or values which the inputs can take make up the search space. The aim of optimization is to find that point or set of points in the search space which gives the optimal solution.

Classification of Optimization Techniques:

Optimization can be classified broadly as:

  • Deterministic: Uses derivative or gradient to reach the final solution. Can be further divided into infinite dimension, finite dimension (non-discrete, discrete; multi-stage, single-stage; linear, non-linear).
  • Stochastic: Derivative-free optimization, a type of random search suitable for non-linearity, discontinuity, and non-convex regions. GAs fall under this category.

Fig. 8.2 Classes of search techniques, showing GAs under Evolutionary Algorithms.

Advantages of GAs:

  • Does not require any derivative information (which may not be available for many real-world problems).
  • Is faster and more efficient as compared to some traditional methods.
  • Has very good parallel capabilities.
  • Optimizes both continuous and discrete functions and also multi-objective problems.
  • Provides a list of "good" solutions and not just a single solution.
  • Always gets an answer to the problem, which gets better over time.
  • Useful when the search space is very large and there are a large number of parameters involved.
  • Exploits historical information well.

Limitations of GAs:

  • GAs are not suited for all problems, especially problems which are simple and for which derivative information is available.
  • Fitness value is calculated repeatedly which might be computationally expensive for some problems.
  • Being stochastic, there are no guarantees on the optimality or the quality of the solution.
  • If not implemented properly (e.g., choice of parameters, operators), the GA may not converge to the optimal solution or may converge prematurely to a local optimum.

2. Genetic Algorithm Basics and Concepts

GAs are good at tackling large, potentially huge, search spaces and navigating them looking for optimal combinations of things and solutions we might not find in a lifetime.

  • GAs need the design space to be converted into a genetic space (coding of variables).
  • A key difference from traditional methods is that GAs use a population of points (solutions) at one time, not a single point. This allows GAs to process a number of designs at the same time.
  • While traditional methods often use deterministic transition rules, GAs use randomized operators (like crossover and mutation) to improve the search space in an adaptive manner.

Three Most Important Aspects of Using GA:

  1. Definition of the objective function (fitness function).
  2. Definition and implementation of genetic representation (encoding).
  3. Definition and implementation of genetic operators (selection, crossover, mutation).

Important Common Aspects of Evolutionary/Swarm Optimization Algorithms:

  1. Search Space: Space for all feasible solutions.
  2. Solution: The point with maximum or minimum value of fitness function.
  3. Fitness Function: A function derived from the objective function, used to evaluate solutions.
  4. Population Size: Number of points in a search space used in parallel for computing, generally ranging from 30 to 200.
  5. Constraints: Lower and upper bounds for variables or conditions.
  6. Stopping Criteria: Number of iterations, minimum value of error in fitness, or minimum improvement from previous iteration.

Creation of Offspring & Search Space:

During creation of offspring, recombination (crossover) and mutation occur. Mutation means an element of DNA is modified. These changes are mainly caused by errors in copying genes. The fitness of an organism is measured by its success in life.

Fig. 8.4 Examples of a search space.

The search space for all possible feasible solutions is where GAs look for extrema (maximum or minimum). GAs are inspired by Darwinian "survival of the fittest." An algorithm starts with a set of solutions (population). Solutions are selected based on fitness to form a new population (offspring), hoping the new population is better. This is repeated until improvement conditions are satisfied.

Biological terms are used to describe parts of a GA:

Anatomy of an Animal Cell and The Cell Nucleus (Illustrative).

Relationship between Chromosome, DNA, and Gene (Illustrative).

  • Cell: Basic unit. Genetic information is in the cell nucleus.
  • Chromosomes: Stores all genetic information, built of DNA. In GAs, a chromosome represents a potential solution to a problem.
  • Genes: A chromosome is divided into genes, each encoding a particular trait or parameter. In GAs, a gene represents a part of the solution (e.g., a specific variable or bit).
  • Allele: Possible values a gene can take (e.g., for eye color: blue, brown). In GAs, the value at a certain gene position.
  • Locus: The position of a gene on the chromosome.
  • Genome: The set of all genes of a specific species. In GAs, the collection of all chromosomes for an individual.
  • Genotype: The entire combination of genes for an individual (the coded representation).
  • Phenotype: The physical aspect or characteristics resulting from the genotype (the decoded solution).
  • Diploid/Haploid: Organisms with paired (diploid) or unpaired (haploid) chromosomes. Most GAs concentrate on haploid chromosomes for simplicity.
  • Gene Pool: The set of all possible alleles present in a particular population.

3. Working Principle of Genetic Algorithms

To illustrate the working principle, we typically consider an unconstrained optimization problem. If f(x) needs to be minimized, and f(x) > 0, the objective function for maximization can be written as: maximize 1 / (1 + f(x)). If f(x) < 0, maximize {-f(x)}.

Components of Genetic Algorithm:

  • Encoding schemes
  • Fitness evaluations
  • Parent selection
  • Crossover operators
  • Mutation operators

After an initial population is randomly generated, the algorithm evolves through three main operators:

  1. Selection: Equates to "survival of the fittest."
  2. Crossover: Represents mating between individuals.
  3. Mutation: Introduces random modifications.

Fig. 3.1.1: One generation of GA process.

Fig. 3.1.2: More detailed GA process flow.

Pseudocode of Genetics Algorithm:

  1. Choose the initial population of individuals.
  2. Evaluate the fitness of each individual in that population.
  3. Repeat until termination condition satisfied:
    1. Selection: Select the individuals with greater fitness for reproduction.
    2. Crossover: Breed new individuals through crossover.
    3. Mutation: Apply probabilistic mutation on new individuals.
    4. Form a new population with these offsprings.
  4. Terminate.

General GA Steps (Outline):

  1. [Start] Generate random population of n chromosomes.
  2. [Fitness] Evaluate the fitness f(x) of each chromosome x in the population.
  3. [New population] Create a new population by repeating the following steps until the new population is complete:
    1. [Selection] Select two parent chromosomes from a population according to their fitness.
    2. [Crossover] With a crossover probability, cross over the parents to form new offspring. If no crossover, offspring is an exact copy.
    3. [Mutation] With a mutation probability, mutate new offspring at each locus.
    4. [Accepting] Place new offspring in a new population.
  4. [Replace] Use new generated population for a further run of algorithm.
  5. [Test] If the end condition is satisfied, stop and return the best solution.
  6. [Loop] Go to step 2.

Fig. 3.1.3: Flow diagram of the genetic algorithm process (simplified).

  1. GAs work with a coded form of the function values (parameter set, e.g., binary strings) rather than the actual values themselves.
  2. GAs use a set or population of points to conduct a search, not just a single point. This allows searching noisy spaces with local optima.
  3. GAs use only payoff (fitness) information to guide themselves, not derivatives or other auxiliary knowledge.
  4. GAs are probabilistic in nature, not deterministic, due to randomized operators.
  5. GAs are inherently parallel, dealing with many points simultaneously.

4. GA Components in Detail

The way candidate solutions are encoded is a central factor in the success of a GA. Most GAs use fixed-length, fixed-order bit strings, but other encodings exist.

  • Binary Encoding: Each chromosome is a string of 0s and 1s. Often used for problems where parameters can be discretized or are naturally binary (e.g., knapsack problem - item selected or not).
  • Permutation Encoding: Each chromosome is a string of numbers that represent a sequence or order. Used in ordering problems like the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), where the chromosome represents the order of cities to visit.
    Example: Chromosome A: 1 5 3 2 6 4 7 9 8
  • Tree Encoding (Genetic Programming): Each chromosome is a tree of objects, such as functions or commands in a programming language. Used for evolving programs or expressions.

    Fig. Example of Chromosomes with tree encoding.

  • Value Encoding (Real-coded GA): Each chromosome is a sequence of some values (real numbers, characters, objects). Direct value encoding can be used where binary encoding is difficult.
    Example: Chromosome A: 1.2324 5.3243 0.4556 ... (for real-valued parameters)
    Example: Chromosome C: (back), (right), (forward), (left) (for a sequence of moves)
    Example (Problem: Finding weights for neural network): Real values in chromosomes represent connection weights.

Selection is usually the first operator applied to a population. It chooses chromosomes from the population to be parents for crossover, based on their fitness. The idea is that better (fitter) solutions should have a higher chance to reproduce.

  • Roulette Wheel Selection:
    • Parents are selected according to their fitness. Better chromosomes have a higher chance of being selected.
    • Imagine a roulette wheel where each chromosome occupies a segment proportional to its fitness value. A marble is thrown, and the chromosome whose segment it lands on is selected.
    • Probability of selecting individual i: p(i) = f(i) / Î£ f(j) for all j in population.
    • Risk of premature convergence if one individual is significantly fitter than others.

      • Roulette Wheel based on fitness values.

  • Boltzmann Selection:
    • A method of functional minimization or maximization that simulates slow cooling of molten metal (simulated annealing concept).
    • The probability of a state E at temperature T is given by P(E) = exp(-E/kT).
    • By controlling temperature T, convergence can be controlled. High T allows more diverse selection; low T favors fitter individuals.
  • Tournament Selection:
    • A strategy to select individuals and insert them into a mating pool.
    • A number (Nu, tournament size, often 2) of individuals are chosen randomly from the population.
    • These Nu individuals "compete," and the one with the highest fitness is selected as a parent.
    • This process is repeated to fill the mating pool.
    • Helps manage selective pressure and population diversity. Higher selective pressure (larger Nu) can lead to faster convergence but also increased risk of premature convergence.

      • Example steps of Tournament Selection.

  • Rank Selection:
    • Ranks individuals based on fitness (best has rank 1, second best rank 2, etc.).
    • Selection probability is based on rank, not absolute fitness values. This can prevent very fit individuals from dominating too early and helps maintain diversity if fitness values differ greatly.
    • Roulette wheel selection can then be applied to these ranks.

      • Fig. 8.16 Roulette-wheel according to rank.

  • Steady-State Selection:
    • Not a particular method of selecting parents, but a replacement strategy.
    • A few (good) individuals with high fitness are selected for creating new offspring. Then, some (bad, low fitness) chromosomes are removed, and new offspring are placed in their place. The rest of the population survives to the next generation.
  • Elitism:
    • The best chromosome (or a few best chromosomes) from the current population are directly copied to the new population, ensuring that the best solution found so far is not lost.
    • Can rapidly increase GA performance but should be used judiciously to avoid premature convergence.
  • Generation Gap and Steady-State Replacement:
    • The generation gap (Gp = p / Np) is the proportion of individuals replaced in each generation (p replaced out of Np total population).
    • Steady-state replacement often has a small generation gap (e.g., only a few individuals replaced).

Crossover is a binary variation operator that mimics biological recombination. It combines information from two parent chromosomes to produce one or more offspring, aiming to generate better solutions from good ones.

Fig. 8.2.1 Basic Genetic Algorithm Operators.

  • Involves choosing a random position(s) in the parent strings (crossover point(s)).
  • Segments are exchanged between parents to produce new offspring.
  • Primary objective is to emphasize good solutions and eliminate bad ones, while keeping population size constant.
  • "Selects The Best, Discards The Rest" principle is more related to selection; crossover combines.
  • The method depends on the encoding scheme.

Crossover Types:

  • 1. One-Point Crossover:
    • A single crossover point is selected randomly along the string length.
    • The parts of the two parent chromosomes beyond this point are swapped to create two offspring.

      • Example of One-Point Crossover.

  • 2. Two-Point Crossover:
    • Two crossover points are selected randomly.
    • The segment between these two points is swapped between the parents to generate two children.
    • More suitable for longer strings than one-point crossover.

      • Example of Two-Point Crossover.

  • 3. Uniform Crossover:
    • Each gene (bit) in the offspring is created by selecting the corresponding gene from one of the parents with a certain probability (e.g., 0.5). A crossover mask (a binary string) can be used to determine which parent contributes which gene.
  • Matrix Crossover (for 2D representations):
    • Substrings (horizontal or vertical) are chosen and swapped. Can be modified to generate multiple offspring.

      • Fig. 3.2.2 & 3.2.3: Horizontal and Vertical substring crossover.

Effects of Genetic Operators:

  • Selection alone: Fills population with copies of the best individual.
  • Selection + Crossover: Tends to converge to good but possibly sub-optimal solutions.
  • Mutation alone: Induces a random walk through the search space.
  • Selection + Mutation: Creates a parallel, noise-tolerant, hill-climbing algorithm.
  • Only crossover can combine information from two parents.
  • Only mutation can introduce new information (alleles).
  • Crossover does not change allele frequencies of the population (in expectation).

Mutation introduces new genetic structures (alleles) in the population by randomly changing some of its building blocks (genes). It helps the algorithm escape local optima and maintain diversity.

  • Randomly flips some bits in a chromosome (for binary encoding) or changes gene values.
  • Helps increase searching power. Each element in an individual is changed with a small probability (Pm, mutation rate).
  • Main purpose is to maintain diversity and inhibit premature convergence.
  • Mutation rate is usually kept low so good chromosomes obtained from crossover are not lost.
  • The type of mutation depends on the encoding. For permutations, it might involve swapping two genes.

Examples of Mutation:

  • Bit Flip Mutation (Binary Encoding): Randomly selected bits are inverted (0 to 1, 1 to 0).
    Example: Offspring: 1101 00100 1 0110 -> Mutated Offspring: 1100 00100 0 0110
  • Exchange Mutation (Permutation Encoding): Two randomly chosen elements (genes) are swapped.
  • Shift Mutation: Movement of a randomly chosen element a random number of places.
  • Scramble Sublist Mutation: Choose two points on the string and randomly permute the elements between these two positions.
  • Flipping based on Mutation Chromosome: A mutation chromosome (binary string) is generated. If a bit in the mutation chromosome is 1, the corresponding bit in the parent is flipped.

Example of Mutation for chromosomes with value encoding and subsequent fitness calculation, showing improvement in average fitness.

Mutation Rate (Pm):

Small Pm values are commonly adopted. It's the probability that a gene will be mutated. It leads to additional genetic diversity to help escape local optima.

Inversion

A string is selected, and the bits between two random sites are inverted (reversed in order).

Fig. 3.3.1: Inversion.

Linear+End inversion operator aims to minimize disruption near the center of the string.

Deletion and Duplication

Low-level operator pair. Two or more bits at random are selected; previous bits might be duplicated or some deleted.

Example:
Before duplication: 0 0 1 0 0 1 0
Deletion (e.g., 3rd bit): 0 0 _ 0 0 1 0
Duplication (e.g., 2nd bit): 0 0 0 1 0 0 1 0 (if 2nd '0' is duplicated)

5. Fitness Function

The fitness function is a crucial component of a GA. It evaluates how "good" each individual (chromosome/solution) in the population is in terms of solving the problem.

  • The fitness of a chromosome determines how likely it is that it will reproduce.
  • It quantifies the optimality of a solution so that it can be ranked against others.
  • An ideal fitness function correlates closely to the goal and is computationally quick.
  • It is always problem-dependent.

Examples:

  • TSP (Traveling Salesman Problem): Fitness(x) is related to the sum of distances between cities in the solution sequence. Lower sum = fitter solution (for minimization).
  • Knapsack Problem: Maximize total value of objects in a knapsack of fixed capacity. Fitness could be the sum of values of objects if the representation is valid (total weight â¤ capacity), or 0 otherwise (or a penalty).
  • Sorting Numbers: Fitness could be determined by how close to a correctly sorted sequence a chromosome is.

The performance of individual strings is measured by the fitness function. After each iteration, members are given a performance measure, and the "fittest" members propagate to the next iteration.

6. Applications of Genetic Algorithms

GAs are primarily used in optimization problems but are also frequently used in other application areas:

  • Optimization: Most common use, to maximize or minimize an objective function under given constraints.
  • Economics: Characterizing economic models like cobweb model, game theory equilibrium, asset pricing.
  • Neural Networks: Training ANNs, particularly recurrent neural networks, or for architecture search.
  • Parallelization: GAs have good parallel capabilities, effective for certain complex problems.
  • Image Processing: Various Digital Image Processing (DIP) tasks like dense pixel matching.
  • Vehicle Routing Problems: With multiple soft time windows, multiple depots, and heterogeneous fleets.
  • Scheduling Applications: Solving various scheduling problems, particularly time-tabling.
  • Machine Learning: Genetics-based machine learning (GBML) is a niche area.
  • Robot Trajectory Generation: Planning paths for robots.
  • Parametric Design of Aircraft: Designing aircraft by varying parameters and evolving better solutions.
  • DNA Analysis: Determining the structure of DNA using spectrometric data.
  • Multimodal Optimization: Finding multiple optimum solutions.
  • Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and its applications: A well-known combinatorial problem.
