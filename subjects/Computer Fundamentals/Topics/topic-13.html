Examining the ethical principles, legal frameworks, societal impacts, and responsible practices associated with computer technology.

Digital Ethics

Computer ethics is a branch of applied ethics that deals with the ethical principles, values, and moral dilemmas arising from the use of computers and related technologies. It involves the study and analysis of ethical issues that arise during the design, development, implementation, and use of computer systems. The field of computer ethics aims to establish guidelines and principles to govern the ethical use of technology and ensure that technology serves the best interests of individuals and society as a whole.

Computer ethics can be defined as the study of ethical issues and concerns related to the use of computer technology. It involves examining how computers impact various aspects of human life, such as privacy, security, intellectual property, freedom of expression, and social interactions. The objective is to identify potential ethical problems and develop guidelines to promote responsible and ethical behavior in the use of computers and technology.

Scope of Computer Ethics:

  • Privacy: Addressing issues of data privacy, surveillance, and the protection of personal information. Ethical frameworks are needed to guide responsible and transparent handling of personal information.
  • Cybersecurity: Examining ethical considerations surrounding cyberattack prevention and responsible use of security measures. Striving for transparency, accountability, and responsible use is crucial.
  • Intellectual Property: Discussing ethical use and protection of copyrights, patents, and trademarks. Ethical guidelines are necessary to foster creativity and promote responsible use.
  • Social Media and Online Behavior: Exploring ethics concerning online communication, cyberbullying, and digital citizenship. Promoting responsible and ethical behavior online is essential.
  • Digital Manipulation and Misinformation: Addressing the ethics of using digital tools to manipulate information (e.g., deepfakes) and spread misinformation. Promoting media literacy and critical thinking is key.

Computer ethics involves a continuous examination of emerging technologies and their potential ethical implications. The field is dynamic and evolves alongside technological advancements, necessitating ongoing discussions and ethical analysis to ensure responsible and beneficial use of computer technology in society.

Cyber Laws

Developers initially didn't foresee the internet's potential for illegal/immoral activities. Grey areas like identity theft, terrorism, money laundering need cyber laws. Criminals exploit DNS, IP, spoofing, phishing to gain unauthorized access and steal data.

Cyber law is a term concerning the legal issues related to the use of communicative, transactional and distributive aspects of networked information devices and technologies. It encompasses legal, statutory and constitutional provisions that affect computer and computer networks. It is a generic term which refers to all the legal and regulatory aspect of internet and World Wide Web. There are currently two main Statutes, which govern online criminal liability - the Indian Penal Code, 1860 and the Information Technology (IT) Act, 2000.

Cyber law is instrumental for:

  1. Entry into cyberspace.
  2. Providing access to cyberspace.
  3. Creating hardware and software to access cyberspace.
  4. Using one's own computers to go "online".

Information Technology Act 2000 (India)

India is the 12th nation in enactment of cyber law with the passage of Information Technology Act, 2000. The United Nations Commission on International Trade Law (UNCITRAL) adopted the model law on electronic commerce in 1996 in order to bring uniformity in the law of different countries. The first draft of legislation was created by the Ministry of Commerce, Government of India as E commerce Act 1998. A redraft of legislation was prepared as “Information Technology Bill 1999” which was placed before the Parliament in December 1999 and passed in May 2000. The main objective of the Act is to regulate and control the affairs of cyber world in an effective manner.

Aim and Objectives of the Act:

  1. To grant legal recognition for transactions carried out by means of electronic data interchange and other means of electronic communication commonly referred as e-commerce in place of paper based method of communication,
  2. To give legal recognition to digital signature for authentication of any information or matter which requires authentication under any law,
  3. To facilitate electronic filing of documents with Government departments,
  4. To facilitate electronic storage of data,
  5. To facilitate and give legal sanction to electronic fund transfer between banks and financial institutions,
  6. To give legal recognition for keeping books of accounts by bankers in electronic form under the Evidence Act 1891 and the Reserve Bank of India Act 1934.

Features of the Act:

  1. Electronic contracts made legally valid through secure electronic communications.
  2. Legal recognition granted to digital signatures.
  3. Security procedures for electronic records and digital signatures laid down.
  4. Procedure for appointment of adjudicating officers.
  5. Provision for establishment of Cyber Regulatory Appellant Tribunal.
  6. Appeals against Controller/Adjudicating officer to Cyber Appellant Tribunal.
  7. Appeals against Cyber Appellant Tribunal to be made in the High court.
  8. Digital signatures effected by asymmetric crypto system and hash function.
  9. Provision for appointment of Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA).
  10. Controller acts as repository for digital signature certificates.
  11. Act applies to offences/contraventions committed outside India.
  12. Senior police officers given power to enter public place and search/arrest without warrant.
  13. Provisions for constitution of Cyber Regulations Advisory Committee.

Applicability and Non-Applicability of the Act:

Applicability: Extends to the whole of India, including Jammu and Kashmir. Applies to offences committed outside India by any person if the conduct involves a computer, computer system or computer network located in India.

Non Applicability (Examples):

  • Execution of Negotiable Instrument (except cheques).
  • Execution of a Power of Attorney.
  • Creation of Trust.
  • Execution of a Will.
  • Entering into a contract for the sale of conveyance of immovable property.
  • Any such class of documents or transactions as may be notified by the Central Government.

Plagiarism, Piracy, Data Misuse

Plagiarism

The word ‘plagiarism’ is derived from the Latin word ‘Plagiare’, which means to kidnap or to abduct. In English, plagiarism refers to the act of using another person’s literary work, whether oral or written, and passing it as your own, without giving credit to the original creator. It is considered as intellectual theft and is a punishable offense. Plagiarism may be unintentional or intentional. According to Merriam-Webster, "to plagiarize" means: to steal and pass off (the ideas or words of another) as one's own, to use (another's production) without crediting the source, to commit literary theft, to present as new and original an idea or product derived from an existing source.

All of the following are considered plagiarism:

  • Turning in someone else’s work as your own.
  • Copying words or ideas from someone else without giving credit.
  • Failing to put a quotation in quotation marks.
  • Giving incorrect information about the source of a quotation.
  • Changing words but copying the sentence structure of a source without giving credit.
  • Copying so many words or ideas from a source that it makes up the majority of your work, whether you give credit or not.

Plagiarism is Offence. If plagiarism involves copying not only ideas but also a substantial portion of a copyrighted work without attribution and without permission, it would amount to both copyright infringement and the violation of the ‘special right’ of the author to be credited.

Methods to Avoid Plagiarism:

Most cases of plagiarism can be avoided by citing sources. You must give credit when: you use another person’s ideas, opinions, or theories; use quotations; paraphrase; use facts, statistics, graphics, drawings, music, etc., that does not comprise common knowledge.

Privacy (in E-commerce)

Privacy is the protection of personal information given online. In e-commerce especially, it is related to a company’s policies on the use of user data. An important factor is consumer consent - whether the consumer is given a choice to decide what the information can and cannot be used for.

How to safeguard User Privacy?

  • Merchant must clearly state how data will be used (terms of site).
  • Merchant must ensure user has gone through terms and conditions.
  • Merchant must assure user about data safety (HTTPS, security measures).
  • User must go through terms and conditions before providing sensitive information and check for HTTPS/padlock.

Data Misuse

Data misuse refers to the unauthorized or improper use of data stored or processed by a computer system. This includes actions like unauthorized access, disclosure, modification, or deletion of data, as well as the use of data for purposes not authorized by the owner or intended by the system.

Examples of Data Misuse:

  • Unauthorized Access
  • Data Theft
  • Data Destruction
  • Data Alteration
  • Data Disclosure
  • Cyber-bullying
  • Identity Theft

Consequences of Data Misuse:

  • Financial Loss
  • Reputational Damage
  • Legal Consequences
  • Privacy Violations
  • System Instability

Preventing Data Misuse:

  • Security Measures (strong passwords, firewalls)
  • Access Control
  • Data Encryption
  • Data Backup and Recovery
  • Awareness Training
  • Monitoring and Auditing

Impact of Technology on Society

1. Impact of technology on business:

Advantage: Competitive advantage, improved services, customer loyalty, better customer service, efficient recruitment. Disadvantage: High cost of technology, maintenance, potential job displacement.

2. Impact of technology on education:

Advantage: Remote learning, access to global courses. Disadvantage: Exposure to unverified data, increased misinformation, potential for exam malpractice if not critically analyzed.

3. Impact of technology on society:

Advantage: Easier movement, improved communication, new relationships. Disadvantage: Digital divide, potential for isolation despite connectivity, cyber-sickness, challenges in distinguishing virtual from real, risks in virtual reality training.

4. Impact of technology on agriculture:

Advantage: Genetically engineered crops with better survival rates and yield. Disadvantage: Initial soil degradation from chemical fertilizers, long-term dependency on artificial fertilizers.

5. Impact of technology on banking:

Advantage: Electronic banking, online transfers, smart cards. Disadvantage: E-cash vulnerability, data interception, hacking risks.

The Digital Divide

Digital Divide refers to the gap between those who benefit from digital technology and those who do not. It includes imbalances in physical access to technology, as well as imbalances in resources and skills needed to effectively participate. Factors contributing to the digital divide gap include:

  • Poverty: Lack of finances to afford computers and internet.
  • Education: Ability to know and understand computer applications is necessary.
  • Gender: Inequality in access and use of ICTs, especially in developing countries.
  • Dependency: Over-reliance on technology for survival and economic activity.
  • Rural and Urban Perspective: Differences in infrastructure, cost, and availability of internet services.
  • Cultural Dimensions: Certain cultures may hinder progress due to resistance to new information and communication tools.
  • Age: Young people generally more comfortable with new technology.
  • Economic Development and Distribution: Unequal access between rich and poor countries.

Environmental Impact (E-waste)

Introduction:

Technological advancements have enhanced quality of life but also led to problems like hazardous waste from electronic products.

E-waste Definition:

Broadly describes loosely discarded, surplus, obsolete, broken, electrical or electronic devices at the end of their useful life. Improper accumulation and recycling can lead to environmental problems.

Source:

IT & telecommunication equipment, consumer electrical/electronic products (refrigerators, washing machines, computers, printers, mobiles, TVs, etc.).

Composition:

Ferrous and non-ferrous metals, plastics, glass, wood, PCBs, ceramics, rubber. Contains heavy metals, polymers, flame retardants.

Generation:

Estimated 50 million tonnes globally per year. Significant generation in India from various sectors.

Environmental and Health Issues:

Serious damage to environment and human/animal health if not properly discarded. Examples: Brominated flame retardants, Lead, Cadmium, Mercury, Hexavalent Chromium, Plastic components.

Treatment and Disposal:

Complex due to hazardous substances. Methods like Land filling, Incineration, Recycling, Re-use.

Regulations:
