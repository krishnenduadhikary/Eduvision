Topic 4

Input and Output Devices

Exploring the various devices used to interact with computers, providing data and instructions (input) and receiving processed information (output).

Input Devices

An input device is used to get data or instructions from the user. This data is then passed on to CPU for processing so as to produce the desired result. Although keyboard and mouse are the two common input devices, other devices such as Optical Character Recognition (OCR), Magnetic Ink Character Recognition (MICR), and mark sense reader, etc., are also used as per our requirement.

Keyboard

The most commonly used input device. Used to type data, text, and execute commands. Consists of groups of keys:

  • Alphanumeric keys: Number and alphabet keys (QWERTY layout).
  • Function keys: Arranged in a row on top (e.g., F1-F12) for specific tasks.
  • Central keys: For cursor control (arrow keys) and modification (SHIFT, ALT, CTRL).
  • Numeric keypad: On the right side, like a calculator.
  • Special purpose keys: Escape, Insert, Delete, Print Screen, Pause, Tab, Spacebar, etc.

A standard keyboard has about 100 keys. Keyboards connect via cable or wirelessly (batteries). Types include multimedia and gaming keyboards based on additional keys.

Mouse

Controls the cursor/pointer on a display screen. A small object that can roll along a hard flat surface. Contains at least one button, sometimes up to three. Commonly connected via USB.

Invented by Douglas Engelbart in 1963. Important for GUIs (point-and-click). Also used for graphics programs.

Types of Mouse:
  • Mechanical: Has a rubber or metal ball on its underside. Mechanical sensors detect ball movement.
  • Optomechanical: Similar to mechanical, but uses optical sensors to detect ball motion.
  • Optical: Uses a laser to detect movement. Often requires a special mat with a grid. No mechanical moving parts, more responsive and precise but more expensive.

Scanner

Reads text or illustrations printed on paper and translates the information into a form the computer can use. Works by digitizing an image (dividing into a grid of boxes, representing each box with 0 or 1). The resulting matrix of bits is a bitmap.

Optical scanners often need an Optical Character Recognition (OCR) system to translate scanned text images into ASCII characters for editing. Most scanners come with OCR packages.

Scanner Differences:
  • Scanning Technology: Most use Charge-Coupled Device (CCD) arrays. Drum scanners use Photo Multiplier Tube (PMT) (more expensive).
  • Resolution: Denser bit map = higher resolution (DPI - Dots Per Inch).
  • Bit depth: Number of bits per pixel (e.g., 24-bit for 16.7 million colors).
  • Size and shape: Hand-held (half-page), sheet-fed (for loose paper), flatbed (like photocopier).

Microphone

An input device used to store voice data into the computer system. A type of transducer (converts energy). Converts sound waves into electrical energy. The diaphragm vibrates when struck by sound waves, causing an electrical current (audio signal).

Microphone Types:
  • Conversion Technology: Dynamic, condenser, ribbon, crystal.
  • Application Design: General use or specialized purpose.
Microphone Diagram

Web Camera (Webcam)

A hardware camera connected to a computer, allowing internet connection to view pictures or motion video. Often embedded in laptops or connected via USB/Wi-Fi. The camera part is a digital camera. Comes with software for preset interval capture and transfer. Allows video use (high frame rate needed for good quality). Captures images repeatedly, stores them in built-in memory, and software converts data to JPEG (compressing format).

Trackball

A pointing device similar to a mouse. A ball is placed on the trackball device, used to move the graphic cursor. Contains buttons for selection. The ball is rolled with fingers or thumb. The device itself doesn't need to move. Often attached to some keyboards. Comes in various shapes (ABALL, SQUARE, A SQUARE).

Advantages:
  • Takes less desk space.
  • Takes less arm movements.
  • Doesn't require a mouse pad.
  • Less strain on the wrist.
  • Finger trip control may offer more accuracy.
Trackball Diagram

Joystick

A pointing device working on the same principle as a trackball. Contains a stick placed on a spherical ball, used to move the cursor. Also has buttons for selection. Pointer moves in the direction the joystick is pointing. Must return joystick to upright position to stop pointer movement.

Systems using joysticks:
  • Aircrafts, UAVs for flight control.
  • Motorized Wheelchairs as input device.
  • Microscopes.
  • Submarines.
  • Security Systems.
  • Video Games (widely used).
Advantages:
  • It is very easy to learn to use.
  • Very simple design so they can be inexpensive.
  • It has a big analogue stick in the middle so it's easier to control.

Optical Character Recognition (OCR)

A device used for reading text from paper and translating the images into a form the computer can understand. Used to convert books, magazines, and other printed information into digital form.

Magnetic Ink Character Recognition (MICR)

Identifies characters printed with a special magnetic ink. Finds applications in the banking industry (e.g., MICR codes on cheques).

Optical Mark Recognition (OMR)

Also called mark sense reader. Senses the presence or absence of a mark (e.g., pencil mark). Widely used for assessing objective examinations involving multiple choice questions.

Bar Code Reader

Photographic scanner that translates bar codes (vertical zebra striped marks) on product containers into codes the computer can understand (Universal Product Code - UPC). Used in supermarkets to automatically tell the Point of Sales (POS) terminal the product and price.

Speech Input Devices

Convert a person's speech into digital form. With appropriate software, form voice recognition systems. Some systems must be 'trained'. Types: Continuous-speech (e.g., Dragon Computer's NaturallySpeaking), Discrete-Word (e.g., IBM VoiceType Simply Speaking).

Touch Screen

A type of display screen that has a touch sensitive transparent panel covering the screen. Instead of using a pointing device, users can point directly to objects on the screen. Provide natural interface for computer novices.

Touch Screen Image

Touch Pad

A small, touch-sensitive pad used as a pointing device on some portable computers. Moving a finger or other object along the pad can move the pointer on the screen.

Light Pen

An input device that utilizes a light-sensitive detector to select objects on a display screen. Similar to a mouse, except that with a light pen we can move the pointer and select objects directly on the display screen by directly pointing to the objects with the light pen.

Output Devices

Output is anything that comes out of a computer. Output can be meaningful information or gibberish and it can appear in a variety of forms - as binary numbers, as characters, as pictures and as printed pages. An output device is any machine capable of representing information from a computer. Output devices include display screens, loudspeakers, printers, plotters etc.

Monitors (Visual Display Unit)

Technically termed as a visual display unit. An electronic device that transmits information from the computer onto a screen. Available in various shapes, designs, and colors. Two types typically used with PCs: CRT (Cathode Ray Tube - older, like a TV screen) and Flat-panel display (used with portable computers, now popular for desktops).

Color Display Categories:
  • Monochrome: Display only one color (e.g., green, amber, white) against a contrasting background (usually black). For text-only displays.
  • Grayscale: Display varying intensities of gray. Essentially a type of monochrome. Used in low-end portable systems.
  • Color: Can display between 16 and 16 million colors. Most new monitors display in color.

LCD Monitors

Liquid Crystal Display (or Diode). Consists of a layer of color or monochrome pixels arranged schematically between transparent electrodes and polarizing filters. Optical effect achieved by polarizing light. Two types of LCD technology: Active matrix (TFT - secure, reliable, better picture) and Passive matrix (slower response, becoming outdated).

Advantages:
  • Compact, lightweight, less desk space.
  • Consume less electricity, can be battery operated.
  • Images do not get geometrically distorted and have little flicker.
Disadvantages:
  • More expensive.
  • Image quality not constant when viewed from different angles.
  • Resolution is always constant; alterations can reduce performance.
LCD Monitor Image

LED Monitors

Light Emitting Diodes. Latest type. A flat panel display using light-emitting diodes for back-lighting instead of Cold Cathode Fluorescent (CCFL) used in LCDs. Primarily, display is LCD, back-lighting is LED.

Advantages:
  • Use much lesser power than CRT and LCD (environmentally friendly).
  • Less negative environmental impact when disposed.
  • Lifespan and durability more than CRT or LCD.
  • Monitor panels can be made very thin.
  • Do not produce much heat.
  • Little expensive than former types.
Types of LED Back-lighting:
  • White-edge LEDs: Fixed around the rim, use diffusion panel.
  • Array of LEDs: Placed behind screen, brightness not controlled individually.
  • Array of LEDs (individual control): Brightness of each LED controlled separately.

Printers

Produces output on paper (hard copy), in text or graphics. Vary in size, speed, and quality.

Types of Printers:
  • Dot Matrix Printer: Uses a print head that moves and strikes an inked ribbon against paper. Characters formed from dots.
  • Ink-jet Printer: Sprays ionized ink on paper. Magnetized plates direct ink. Better print than dot matrix (e.g., 300 dpi). Slower than laser, special paper for no smudging. Popular as portable printers. Good for full-colour documents.
  • Laser Printer: Works on photocopier principle. Laser alters electrical charge on a drum, toner picked up by charged portions, transferred to paper with heat/pressure. Often called page printers. Good for graphics. Speed: 4-20 ppm.
  • Thermal Printer: Pushes electrically heated pins against special heat-sensitive paper. Inexpensive. Used in calculators, fax machines. Low-quality print, paper curls/fades.
Ink-jet Printer Image

Speakers

Used to produce audio output. Computers have sound cards to enable audio output through speakers. 3D audio gives more depth to traditional stereo by placing a device in a room with stereo speakers; device dynamically analyses sound and sends feedback to sound system.

Sound Card and Speakers Image

Projectors

A device connected to a computer or video device for projecting an image onto a big white screen. Images are larger than original. Consists of an optic system, light source, and displays. Classified into three categories:

  • Ultralight portable projectors
  • Conference room projectors
  • Fixed installation projectors

Initially for films, now for presentations, business organizations, home theatres. Fixed installation projectors are most expensive, used for projecting in bright light.
