Classification & Generations of Computers

Explore how computers are categorized based on their working principles and size, and trace their evolution through different technological generations.

1.6 Classification of Computers (by Working Principle)

Analog Computer

An analog computer is a form of computer that uses the continuously changeable aspects of physical phenomena such as electrical, mechanical, or hydraulic quantities to model the problem being solved.

Digital Computer

A digital computer is a device capable of solving problems by processing information in discrete form. It operates on data, including magnitudes, letters, and symbols, that are expressed in binary form—i.e., using only the two digits 0 and 1.

Hybrid Computer

Hybrid computers are computers that exhibit features of analog computers and digital computers. The digital component normally serves as the controller and provides logical and numerical operations, while the analog component often serves as a solver of differential equations and other mathematically complex equations.

1.6 Types of Computers (by Size/Power/Purpose)

Present day computers can be categorized as below:

Supercomputer

Fastest and very expensive. Used for specialized applications requiring immense mathematical calculations, like weather forecasting, animated graphics, fluid dynamic calculations, nuclear energy research, and petroleum exploration.

Mainframe Computer

Very large and expensive, capable of supporting hundreds or thousands of users simultaneously. In hierarchy, mainframes are just below supercomputers. Mainframes can execute many programs concurrently, while supercomputers excel at executing a single program faster.

Mini Computer

A mid-sized computer in size and power, lying between workstations and mainframes. A minicomputer is a multiprocessing system capable of supporting from 4 to about 200 users simultaneously. The distinction between large minicomputers and small mainframes has blurred.

Microcomputer: Desktop

A personal or micro-mini computer sufficient to fit on a desk.

Microcomputer: Laptop/Notebook

A portable computer complete with an integrated screen and keyboard. Generally smaller in size than a desktop computer and larger than a notebook computer (though terms are often used interchangeably).

Microcomputer: Palmtop/PDA

Hand-sized computers like Palmtops, Digital Diaries, Notebooks, or Personal Digital Assistants (PDAs). Their screen often serves as both an input and output device. Some might have a small keyboard.

Microcomputer: Tablet PC

Mobile PCs combining features of laptops and handhelds. They have a built-in screen and allow users to write notes or draw pictures with a stylus. Some are "convertibles" with screens that swivel.

Workstation

A terminal or desktop computer in a network. In this context, a workstation is a generic term for a user's machine (client machine) in contrast to a "server" or "mainframe."

Embedded Systems

Special-purpose devices where the computer is embedded. Examples include video game players, microwave ovens, smart-watches, programmable thermostats, car engine controllers, CD players. Typically given one task.

1.3 Generations of Computers

Over the years, various computing devices were invented that enabled people to solve different types of problems. These computing devices can be classified into several generations, referring to the phases of improvement made to different computing devices. Technological development in computers refers not only to hardware technologies but also to software technologies. Generation in computer terminology is a change in technology a computer is/was being used. Initially, the term was used to distinguish between varying hardware technologies. But nowadays, generation includes both hardware and software, which together make up an entire computer system.

There are totally five computer generations known till date. Here approximate dates against each generation have been mentioned which are normally accepted.

First Generation (1946-1959)

Key Technology: Vacuum Tubes. Punched cards, paper tape, and magnetic tape were used as input/output devices. Programming in machine language.

  • Generally special-purpose.

Examples:

ENIAC, EDVAC, UNIVAC, EDSAC, IBM-701, IBM-650.

Second Generation (1959-1965)

Key Technology: Transistors. Magnetic cores for primary memory, magnetic tape and disks for secondary storage. Assembly language and early high-level languages (FORTRAN, COBOL). Batch processing and multiprogramming operating systems.

Placeholder for Transistor Diagram & 2nd Gen Computer Image

Main Features:

  • Use of transistors.
  • More reliable in comparison to first-generation computers.
  • Smaller size as compared to first-generation computers.
  • Generated less heat.
  • Consumed less electricity.
  • Faster than first-generation computers.
  • Still very costly.
  • A.C. needed.
  • Supported machine and assembly languages.
  • Could be transferred (smaller and lighter).

Limitations:

  • Input/output media for these computers were not improved to a considerable extent.
  • Required to be placed in air-conditioned places.

Examples:

IBM 1620, IBM 7094 (or 7090), CDC 1604, CDC 3600, UNIVAC 1108, PDP-8, IBM 1401.

Third Generation (1965-1971)

Key Technology: Integrated Circuits (ICs) - SSI and MSI. Use of ICs led to smaller, faster, more reliable, and efficient computers. High-level languages (FORTRAN-II TO IV, COBOL, PASCAL PL/1, BASIC, ALGOL-68). Time-sharing, multi-programming, remote processing operating systems.

Main Features:

  • IC used.
  • More reliable in comparison to previous two generations.
  • Smaller size.
  • Generated less heat.
  • Faster (computational time in nanoseconds).
  • Lesser maintenance.
  • Still costly.
  • A.C needed.
  • Consumed lesser electricity.
  • Supported high-level language.
  • General-purpose computers.
  • Easily transportable.

Disadvantages:

  • The storage capacity of these computers was still very small.
  • The performance of these computers degraded while solving complex computations.

Examples:

IBM-360 series, Honeywell-6000 series, PDP (Personal Data Processor), IBM-370/168, TDC-316, NCR 395, B6500, CDC 7600.

Fourth Generation (1971-1980)

Key Technology: Microprocessors using Large Scale Integration (LSI) and Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) technology. Rise of Personal Computers (PC). Development of GUIs, networks (LANs), and more advanced OS (time-sharing, real-time, distributed). High-level languages like C, C++, DBASE.

Main Features:

  • VLSI technology used.
  • Very cheap.
  • Portable and reliable.
  • Use of PCs.
  • Very small size.
  • Pipeline processing.

Problems:

  • Soldering of LSI and VLSI chips on the wiring board was not an easy task and required complicated technologies to bind these chips on the wiring board.
  • The working of these computers is still dependent on the instructions given by the programmer.

Examples:

DEC 10, STAR 1000, PDP 11, CRAY-1 (Supercomputer), CRAY-X-MP (Supercomputer), IBM PC, Apple series, Intel 386, 486, Pentium.

Fifth Generation (1980-Onwards)

Key Technology: Ultra Large Scale Integration (ULSI) technology, resulting in microprocessor chips with ten million electronic components. Focus on Artificial Intelligence (AI), parallel processing, superconductor technology, optical disk storage, and the Internet.
