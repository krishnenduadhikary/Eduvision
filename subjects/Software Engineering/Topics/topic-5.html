Topic 5

Requirements Analysis & Specification

Software requirements are detailed descriptions of the features, functionalities, and constraints of the target system. They articulate user expectations and form the foundation for successful software development. Requirements can range from obvious to hidden, known to unknown, and expected to unexpected from the client's perspective.

Requirement Engineering: This is the systematic process of gathering software requirements from clients, analyzing them, and meticulously documenting them. The primary goal is to develop and maintain a comprehensive and descriptive 'System Requirements Specification' (SRS) document.

Requirement Engineering Process

This is a structured, four-step process crucial for defining a clear path for development:

  1. Feasibility Study

    When a client approaches an organization for software development, they typically provide a high-level idea of the desired functions and features. Analysts then conduct a detailed study to determine if developing the proposed system and its functionalities is viable. This study considers:

    • Alignment with organizational goals.
    • Practicality of implementation.
    • Potential contribution of the project to the organization.
    • Cost constraints and budget adherence.
    • Technical aspects like usability, maintainability, productivity, and integration capabilities.

    The output is a feasibility study report, offering recommendations to management on whether to proceed with the project.

  2. Requirement Gathering (Elicitation)

    If the feasibility study is positive, this phase begins. Analysts and engineers engage directly with the client and end-users to understand their vision for the software, including desired functionalities and features. This involves various techniques to extract all relevant information.

  3. Software Requirement Specification (SRS)

    After requirements are collected, system analysts create an SRS document. This document formalizes the requirements in a structured manner.

  4. Software Requirement Validation

    Once the SRS is developed, the specified requirements are validated. This step is critical because users might request impractical solutions, or experts might misinterpret needs, leading to significant cost overruns if not addressed early. Requirements are checked for conditions like:

    • Practical implementability.
    • Validity concerning software functionality and domain.
    • Absence of ambiguities.
    • Completeness.
    • Demonstrability (can it be shown to work?).

Requirement Elicitation Process

The process of discovering, reviewing, documenting, and understanding the user's needs and constraints for the system. It's a critical early step within Requirement Gathering.

  • Requirements Gathering: Developers engage with clients and end-users to understand their expectations and needs from the software. This involves interviews, workshops, surveys, etc.
  • Organizing Requirements: Developers prioritize and arrange the gathered requirements based on importance, urgency, and convenience. This helps in structuring the vast amount of information.
  • Negotiation & Discussion: If requirements are ambiguous, conflicting, or unrealistic, they are discussed and negotiated with stakeholders. Requirements may be prioritized or reasonably compromised to achieve clarity and correctness.
  • Documentation (leading to Specification): All formal and informal, functional and non-functional requirements are meticulously documented. This documentation forms the basis for the SRS and subsequent development phases.

Software Requirements Specification (SRS)

An SRS is a comprehensive document created by a system analyst after collecting requirements from various stakeholders. It serves as a blueprint for development, defining how the intended software will interact with hardware, external interfaces, and users, along with performance criteria, portability, maintainability, security, and other quality attributes.

While requirements from clients are often in natural language, the SRS translates these into a more structured, technical language understandable by the development team.

Key Features of an SRS:

  • User Requirements are expressed in natural language for client understanding.
  • Technical requirements are expressed in a structured language used within the organization.
  • Design descriptions may be written in pseudocode.
  • Includes formats of forms and GUI screen prints where applicable.
  • May use conditional and mathematical notations for Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs) and other models.

Purpose of an SRS Document:

  • Establishes clear communication between the Customer, Analyst, system developers, and maintainers.
  • Provides a firm foundation for the design phase.
  • Supports system testing activities by defining expected outcomes.
  • Aids in project management and control.
  • Facilitates controlled evolution of the system by documenting agreed-upon functionalities.

An SRS is a detailed description of a software system to be developed, encompassing both its functional and non-functional requirements. It's based on the agreement between the customer and contractors and often includes use cases illustrating user interactions. A clear understanding of the SRS is vital for QA teams to create effective test plans and avoid faults.

Characteristics of a Good SRS:

A high-quality SRS document exhibits several key characteristics that contribute to project success. The diagram below illustrates these qualities, followed by detailed explanations.

Correctness

Ensures all stated requirements accurately reflect what is expected from the system. User reviews are key to verifying correctness.

Completeness

Indicates all aspects are covered, including page numbering, resolution of "To Be Determined" (TBD) sections, and comprehensive coverage of functional and non-functional requirements.

Consistency

Requires no conflicts between any set of requirements. Terminology should be uniform, and logical contradictions (e.g., in report generation times) must be avoided.

Unambiguousness

Means each requirement has only one interpretation. This prevents misunderstandings. Modeling techniques like ER diagrams and peer reviews help achieve this.

Ranking for Importance & Stability

Requirements should be classified (e.g., essential, desirable) to indicate their priority and stability (likelihood of change).

Modifiability

The SRS should be structured to easily accept changes. Modifications should be properly indexed and cross-referenced.

Verifiability

Each requirement should be quantifiable and testable. Vague statements like "user-friendly" should be avoided or refined into measurable criteria.

Traceability

Allows tracing a requirement to design components, code segments, and corresponding test cases, and vice-versa.

Design Independence

The SRS should focus on *what* the system must do, not *how* it will be implemented, allowing flexibility in design choices.

Testability

Written to facilitate the easy generation of test cases and test plans from the document.

Understandable by the Customer

Uses clear, simple language, minimizing formal notations and technical jargon to ensure end-users can comprehend it.

Right Level of Abstraction

The level of detail should match the purpose of the SRS (e.g., more detail for the requirements phase, less for a feasibility study).

Parts of an SRS Document

An SRS document typically includes the following main sections:

  1. Functional Requirements

    These describe the functionalities the system must perform – what the system should *do*. They detail the interactions between the system and its environment and specify the inputs, outputs, processing, and behavior under various conditions. Each function (Fi) can be seen as transforming a set of inputs (Ii) into a set of outputs (Oi).

    These requirements define how the user will get a purposeful piece of labor done by employing the system.

  2. Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs)

    These describe the qualities or constraints of the system, rather than specific functions. They define *how well* the system should perform its functions. NFRs accommodate characteristics not directly expressed as functions, such as:

    • Maintainability of the system
    • Movability (Portability) of the system
    • Usability of the system
    • Reliability issues (e.g., Mean Time Between Failures - MTBF)
    • Accuracy of results
    • Human-computer interface issues (e.g., ease of learning, response time)
    • Constraints on system implementation (e.g., programming language, database choice)
    • Performance (e.g., response time, throughput)
    • Security (e.g., access control, data encryption)
    • Scalability
  3. Goals of Implementation

    This section documents general suggestions and trade-offs related to development. It guides decisions about style, potential future revisions (e.g., new devices to be supported), reusability concerns, and other long-term considerations. These are aspects developers should keep in mind throughout development to ensure the system not only meets current needs but is also adaptable for the future.

Formal System Development Techniques

While many requirements are captured in natural language or semi-formal models, formal methods use mathematical notations and techniques to specify and verify system properties. These techniques aim for precision, unambiguity, and the ability to prove correctness.

Two common categories of formal specification include:

  • Axiomatic Specification:

    This approach defines a system's behavior by stating axioms (pre-conditions and post-conditions) about its operations. Pre-conditions describe the state before an operation is executed, and post-conditions describe the state after. Hoare logic is a well-known example of an axiomatic approach, often used for program verification.

  • Algebraic Specification:

    This technique specifies a system (often abstract data types) by defining its operations and the algebraic relationships (equations or axioms) between these operations. It focuses on the "what" (the observable behavior) rather than the "how" (the implementation). For example, a stack can be defined by operations like `push`, `pop`, `top`, `isEmpty`, and axioms like `pop(push(s, x)) = s`.

Formal methods can be complex and require specialized expertise but offer significant benefits in developing highly reliable and safety-critical systems by enabling rigorous analysis and verification of requirements and design.
